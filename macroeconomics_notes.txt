MACROECONOMICS NOTES (FORMULAS + DERIVATIONS + THEORY + IMPORTANT POINTERS)

Notation and conventions used in this document:
- e is in direct quotation: domestic currency per unit of foreign currency.
- pi denotes inflation rate and pi^e denotes expected inflation.
- Time subscripts use parentheses, e.g., X(t), X(t-1).
- beta denotes Phillips-curve sensitivity of inflation to the unemployment gap.
- alpha_K denotes capital share in production-function growth accounting.
- Asterisk (*) on a variable denotes the foreign-economy counterpart (e.g., P*).

========================================================================
PART A: COMPREHENSIVE FORMULA NOTES WITH DERIVATIONS
========================================================================

1) NATIONAL INCOME ACCOUNTING
-----------------------------

1.1 GDP (Expenditure Method)
Formula:
GDP = C + I + G + (X - M)

Derivation idea:
Total output produced domestically is purchased by:
- Households (C)
- Firms (I)
- Government (G)
- Foreign sector through net exports (X - M)
Adding all spending on final goods/services gives GDP.

1.2 GDP (Income Method)
Formula:
GDP = Wages + Rent + Interest + Profit + Indirect Taxes - Subsidies + Depreciation

Derivation idea:
Every unit of output sold becomes someone’s income. Summing factor incomes and adjusting for taxes/subsidies and depreciation gives GDP at market prices.

1.3 Value Added Method
Formula:
GDP = Sum of value added at each production stage
where Value Added = Value of Output - Intermediate Consumption

Derivation idea:
Adding gross output across firms double-counts intermediates. Value added removes this double counting.

1.4 GNP / NNP Relations
Formulas:
GNP = GDP + NFIA
NNP = GNP - Depreciation
NDP = GDP - Depreciation
NNP at FC = NNP at MP - Net Indirect Taxes

where:
NFIA = Net Factor Income from Abroad
Net Indirect Taxes (NIT) = Indirect Taxes - Subsidies

Derivation chain:
Domestic output (GDP) + net foreign factor income = national output (GNP).
Subtract capital consumption (depreciation) to get net concepts.
Convert MP to FC by removing net indirect taxes.

1.5 Personal Income and Disposable Income
Formulas:
PI = NI - Undistributed Profits - Corporate Tax - Social Security Contributions + Transfer Payments
DI = PI - Personal Direct Taxes

Derivation idea:
Not all national income reaches households as personal income. Adjust for retained earnings/taxes/contributions/transfers, then subtract personal taxes.

========================================================================
2) PRICE INDICES, INFLATION, REAL VS NOMINAL
========================================================================

2.1 GDP Deflator
Formula:
GDP Deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

Derivation idea:
Nominal GDP uses current prices; real GDP uses base-year prices.
Their ratio isolates price-level change.

2.2 Consumer Price Index (CPI)
Formula:
CPI = (Cost of current basket at current prices / Cost of same basket at base-year prices) × 100

2.3 Inflation Rate
Formula:
Inflation(t) = [(Price Index(t) - Price Index(t-1)) / Price Index(t-1)] × 100

========================================================================
3) KEYNESIAN GOODS MARKET (CROSS MODEL)
========================================================================

3.1 Consumption Function
Formula:
C = C0 + cYd
where:
C0 = autonomous consumption
c = MPC (0 < c < 1)
Yd = disposable income

3.2 Saving Function
From Yd = C + S:
S = Yd - C = Yd - (C0 + cYd) = -C0 + (1 - c)Yd
Hence MPS = 1 - c

3.3 Equilibrium Income (Two-Sector Closed Economy)
Condition:
Y = C + I
Y = C0 + cY + I
Y(1 - c) = C0 + I
Equilibrium:
Y* = (1 / (1 - c)) (C0 + I)

3.4 Multiplier
Income multiplier:
k = 1 / (1 - MPC) = 1 / MPS

Derivation:
Delta A = change in autonomous spending
If autonomous spending rises by Delta A, then:
Delta Y = Delta A + c * Delta A + c^2 * Delta A + ...
Geometric sum gives Delta Y = Delta A / (1 - c)
So k = Delta Y / Delta A = 1 / (1 - c).

3.5 Government Sector (Lump-Sum Taxes)
If:
C = C0 + c(Y - T)
and equilibrium:
Y = C + I + G
then:
Y = C0 + c(Y - T) + I + G
Y(1 - c) = C0 - cT + I + G
Y* = (1 / (1 - c))(C0 - cT + I + G)

Government expenditure multiplier:
kG = 1 / (1 - c)
Tax multiplier:
kT = -c / (1 - c)
Balanced budget multiplier:
kBB = kG + kT = 1

========================================================================
4) MONEY, BANKING, AND MONETARY FORMULAS
========================================================================

4.1 Quantity Theory (Simple)
MV = PY
If V is stable and Y near potential, growth in M largely affects P.

4.2 Demand for Money (Liquidity Preference Form)
Md = L(Y, i)
Common linear form:
Md = kY - hi
where k > 0, h > 0

4.3 Money Multiplier (Basic)
If reserve ratio = rr and no leakages:
m = 1 / rr
Money supply:
M = m × H
where H = high-powered money (monetary base)

With currency-deposit ratio cdr and excess reserve ratio e:
m = (1 + cdr) / (cdr + rr + e)

Derivation:
M = C + D = (1 + cdr)D
H = C + R = cdrD + (rr + e)D = (cdr + rr + e)D
So m = M/H = (1 + cdr)/(cdr + rr + e)

========================================================================
5) IS-LM FRAMEWORK (SHORT RUN)
========================================================================

5.1 IS Curve (Goods Market Equilibrium)
Y = C(Y - T) + I(i) + G
Downward slope in (Y, i): higher i lowers I, reducing equilibrium Y.

5.2 LM Curve (Money Market Equilibrium)
M/P = L(Y, i)
Upward slope in (Y, i): higher Y raises money demand, requiring higher i for equilibrium given M/P.

5.3 Policy in IS-LM
- Fiscal expansion (↑G or ↓T): IS shifts right.
- Monetary expansion (↑M or ↓money demand): LM shifts right/down.

========================================================================
6) OPEN ECONOMY BASICS
========================================================================

6.1 Net Exports
NX = X - M

6.2 National Income Identity (Open Economy)
Y = C + I + G + NX

6.3 Savings-Investment Relation
From identity:
S - I = NX  (in simplified setup)
If NX > 0, country lends abroad; if NX < 0, borrows from abroad.

6.4 Real Exchange Rate (q)
q = e * (P* / P)
where:
e = nominal exchange rate (domestic currency per foreign currency),
P* = foreign price level, P = domestic price level.

========================================================================
7) UNEMPLOYMENT AND PHILLIPS CURVE
========================================================================

7.1 Unemployment Rate
u = (Number of Unemployed / Labor Force) × 100

7.2 Labor Force Participation Rate
LFPR = (Labor Force / Working-age Population) × 100

7.3 Expectations-Augmented Phillips Curve
pi = pi^e - beta(u - un) + v
where:
pi = actual inflation rate, pi^e = expected inflation rate
beta = sensitivity of inflation to unemployment gap (beta > 0)
un = natural rate of unemployment
v = supply shock

Interpretation:
When u < un, inflation tends to exceed expected inflation.

========================================================================
8) GROWTH BASICS
========================================================================

8.1 Per Capita Output
y = Y/N

8.2 Growth Rate Approximation
gY ≈ gA + alpha_K gK + (1 - alpha_K) gL   (Cobb-Douglas context)

8.3 Rule of 70
Doubling time (years) ≈ 70 / annual growth rate (%)

========================================================================
PART B: COMPREHENSIVE THEORY NOTES + IMPORTANT POINTERS
========================================================================

1) CORE MACRO QUESTIONS
-----------------------
Macroeconomics studies economy-wide outcomes:
- Output/income (GDP, GNP)
- Inflation
- Unemployment
- Interest rates
- Growth over time
- External balance (trade and capital flows)

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Always identify whether the question is:
- short run vs long run,
- closed vs open economy,
- nominal vs real variable.

2) CIRCULAR FLOW AND NATIONAL ACCOUNTS
--------------------------------------
- Households supply factors; firms pay incomes and produce goods.
- Government taxes/spends; foreign sector trades goods/services/factors.
- Three GDP approaches (expenditure/income/value-added) should match in principle.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Avoid double counting by using final goods or value added only.

3) NOMINAL VS REAL DISTINCTION
------------------------------
- Nominal values use current prices.
- Real values remove price effects (constant prices).
- Inflation is sustained rise in general price level.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
For welfare and growth comparisons, prefer real per-capita variables.

4) KEYNESIAN SHORT-RUN DEMAND DETERMINATION
-------------------------------------------
- In short run, output can be demand-constrained.
- Autonomous spending drives induced spending through multiplier.
- MPC high => larger multiplier => stronger demand effects.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Multiplier works strongly only when idle capacity exists and leakages are limited.

5) FISCAL POLICY
----------------
Tools:
- Government spending
- Taxation
- Transfers

Effects:
- Expansionary fiscal policy raises aggregate demand.
- Contractionary policy lowers demand/inflation pressure.

IMPORTANT POINTERS:
- Crowding out can reduce fiscal impact when interest rates rise.
- Balanced-budget change can still raise output (multiplier = 1 in simple model).
- Fiscal sustainability depends on debt dynamics, growth, and interest rates.

6) MONEY AND MONETARY POLICY
----------------------------
- Central bank controls policy rate and influences liquidity.
- Transmission channels: interest rate, credit, expectations, exchange rate, asset prices.
- Tight policy controls inflation but can slow growth in short run.

IMPORTANT POINTERS:
- Distinguish policy rate changes from money supply changes.
- Real interest rate (approximately i - pi^e) is key for spending decisions.
- Time lags in monetary transmission are significant.

7) IS-LM INTUITION
------------------
- IS: goods-market combinations of Y and i.
- LM: money-market combinations of Y and i.
- Intersection gives short-run equilibrium.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Policy effect size depends on curve slopes:
- Flat LM => fiscal policy strong
- Flat IS => monetary policy strong

8) AGGREGATE DEMAND-AGGREGATE SUPPLY (AD-AS)
--------------------------------------------
- AD slopes downward due to real balances, interest rate, and exchange rate channels.
- SRAS upward sloping; LRAS vertical at potential output.
- Demand shocks move output and prices in short run.
- Supply shocks can create inflation + output loss (stagflation).

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Do not treat inflation from demand shocks and supply shocks as identical; policy trade-offs differ.

9) UNEMPLOYMENT TYPES
---------------------
- Frictional: search/matching time
- Structural: skill/location mismatch
- Cyclical: due to low aggregate demand

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Only cyclical unemployment is directly targeted by demand stabilization policy.

10) INFLATION: SOURCES AND COSTS
--------------------------------
Sources:
- Demand-pull
- Cost-push
- Built-in (expectations/wage-price dynamics)

Costs:
- Menu/shoe-leather effects
- Arbitrary redistribution
- Uncertainty and planning distortions

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Expected and unexpected inflation have different real effects.

11) OPEN ECONOMY LOGIC
----------------------
- Trade balance linked to savings-investment gap.
- Exchange-rate regime (fixed/floating) alters policy autonomy.
- Capital mobility changes fiscal/monetary effectiveness.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Always track whether appreciation/depreciation is nominal or real.

12) LONG-RUN GROWTH THEMES
--------------------------
- Capital accumulation, labor growth, and productivity (TFP) drive output.
- Technology, institutions, human capital, and openness strongly affect TFP.
- Convergence may occur conditionally (not automatically).

IMPORTANT POINTERS:
- Sustained long-run growth is mainly productivity-led, not only demand-led.
- Per-capita growth matters more for living standards than total GDP growth.

13) BUSINESS CYCLES AND STABILIZATION
-------------------------------------
- Economies fluctuate around trend (expansion, peak, contraction, trough).
- Countercyclical policy aims to reduce volatility.

IMPORTANT POINTER:
Policy design must consider lags:
- Recognition lag
- Decision lag
- Implementation lag
- Impact lag

14) EXAM/REVISION QUICK POINTERS
--------------------------------
- Start every problem by writing the relevant identity first.
- Check units: level vs growth rate vs percentage point change.
- In derivations, state assumptions explicitly (closed/open economy, fixed prices, lump-sum tax, etc.).
- For diagrams: label axes, initial equilibrium, curve shifts, and new equilibrium.
- For policy answers: include side effects, limits, and time horizon.

END OF NOTES
